# 1. What the Mini Mock Test Measures

The real MET measures how well you use English in real-world personal, public, educational, and professional situations. This shorter mock keeps the same general spirit: you read, listen, choose answers, and write clearly under time pressure.

According to Michigan Language Assessment, MET is a four-skill test. Listening and Reading questions are multiple choice with one correct answer. Writing has two tasks. Listening checks main idea, details, and implied meaning. Reading includes reading comprehension and grammar.

In this mini mock, your goal is not only to get the answer right. Your goal is to notice **why** you got it right or wrong.

## B2 performance target

At B2 level, you should be able to:

- understand the main points of clear texts and speech;
- follow arguments on familiar topics;
- express opinions with reasons;
- use grammar and vocabulary with enough accuracy for clear communication.

## Skills practiced in this mock

| Skill | What you need to do |
|---|---|
| Reading | Identify purpose, main idea, important details, and implied meaning. |
| Use of English | Choose accurate grammar and natural vocabulary in context. |
| Writing | Answer the task directly with organized paragraphs and connected ideas. |
| Listening | Understand purpose, attitude, specific information, and conclusions. |

## Your best strategy for the whole test

| Before answering | While answering | After answering |
|---|---|---|
| Read the instructions and predict what skill is being tested. | Underline key words and eliminate clearly wrong options. | Check that your answer matches the text or audio, not only your opinion. |
| Notice time limits so you do not spend too long on one item. | Use context. One word rarely decides the answer alone. | For mistakes, write the reason: grammar, vocabulary, detail, inference, or time. |

# 2. Reading Comprehension

In the mini mock, Reading includes short texts such as emails, announcements, articles, or notices. The questions test comprehension, not memorization. You need to understand what the writer says, why they say it, and what can reasonably be inferred.

## Reading question types

| Type | What it asks | Common question words | Best strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Main idea | What the whole text is mostly about. | main purpose, mainly, best title | Read the first and last sentences. Avoid options that are too narrow. |
| Detail | A fact directly stated in the text. | according to, what, when, where, why | Locate the exact part of the text. Watch for paraphrases. |
| Inference | What is suggested but not said directly. | suggests, implies, probably, can be inferred | Use evidence from the text. Do not guess from personal opinion. |
| Vocabulary in context | The meaning of a word or phrase in the passage. | closest in meaning, refers to | Replace the word with each option and check if the sentence still makes sense. |
| Purpose / tone | Why the writer wrote it or how they feel. | purpose, tone, attitude | Look for intention: inform, warn, persuade, recommend, complain. |

## Reading examples

### Example 1 - Main idea

**Mini text:**  
The community center will close early on Friday because staff will attend emergency training. Regular hours resume Monday.

**Question:** What is the main purpose of the notice?

A. To cancel all classes  
B. To explain a schedule change  
C. To advertise training  
D. To hire new staff

**Answer:** B.  
**Why:** The notice mainly explains that the center will close early and when normal hours return.

### Example 2 - Detail

**Mini text:**  
The workshop is free, but participants must register online by Wednesday to receive materials.

**Question:** What must participants do by Wednesday?

A. Pay a fee  
B. Call the organizer  
C. Register online  
D. Pick up materials

**Answer:** C.  
**Why:** The text directly says participants must register online by Wednesday.

### Example 3 - Inference

**Mini text:**  
Although Marta had prepared carefully, she asked for a few extra minutes before beginning her presentation.

**Question:** What can be inferred about Marta?

A. She had not prepared  
B. She was probably a little nervous  
C. She wanted to cancel  
D. She disliked presentations

**Answer:** B.  
**Why:** Asking for extra time even after preparation suggests nervousness, not lack of preparation.

## How to answer Reading items

1. Read the question first and decide the skill: main idea, detail, inference, vocabulary, or purpose.
2. Scan the text for the relevant sentence or paragraph.
3. Underline evidence. The correct answer must be supported by the text.
4. Eliminate distractors: too extreme, not mentioned, opposite meaning, or true but not answering the question.
5. Choose the best answer, not the answer that sounds generally true.

> **Common Reading mistake:** Students often choose an option because it is true in real life. In MET-style reading, the answer must come from the text. Evidence first, opinion second.

# 3. Use of English

This section checks whether you can choose grammar and vocabulary that fit the sentence naturally. In the real MET Reading section, grammar items are multiple choice with a missing word or phrase. In this mini mock, the Use of English section gives similar focused practice.

## Use of English exercise types

| Type | What to check | Mini example | Answer |
|---|---|---|---|
| Verb tenses | Time markers and sequence | By the time we arrived, the meeting ___. A. started B. had started C. has started D. starts | B. had started |
| Conditionals | Real vs hypothetical meaning | If I had more time, I ___ the report again. A. review B. reviewed C. would review D. will review | C. would review |
| Passive voice | Focus on action/result, not doer | The new policy ___ next month. A. will introduce B. will be introduced C. introduced D. has introducing | B. will be introduced |
| Reported speech | Backshift and pronouns | She said she ___ the email earlier. A. sends B. has sent C. had sent D. will send | C. had sent |
| Word formation | Part of speech | The manager gave a clear ___ of the changes. A. explain B. explained C. explanation D. explanatory | C. explanation |
| Phrasal verbs | Meaning in context | The nurse asked him to ___ the form before the appointment. A. fill out B. put off C. take after D. run into | A. fill out |
| Collocations | Words that naturally go together | The company made a ___ effort to reduce waste. A. strong B. heavy C. large D. high | A. strong |

## How to answer Use of English items

1. Read the whole sentence before checking the options.
2. Identify the grammar signal: time expression, subject, object, reporting verb, or preposition.
3. Check meaning and structure together. A grammar form can be correct but unnatural in context.
4. For vocabulary, test collocation: Does this word usually go with the noun or verb?
5. For word formation, decide the needed part of speech: noun, verb, adjective, or adverb.

## Fast B2 grammar check

Ask yourself:

- What time is it?
- Who does the action?
- Is the meaning real, hypothetical, or reported?
- What part of speech is missing?
- Which word sounds natural with the noun or verb?

# 4. Writing

The Writing section checks whether you can communicate clearly, organize ideas, support your points, and use accurate language. In the real MET, Writing has two tasks. In this mini mock, the writing section gives shorter practice with the same core skills.

## Writing Task 1 - Short responses or email-style answers

Task 1 usually requires direct answers to related questions. Your answers should be clear, specific, and complete.

### Task 1 example prompt

You recently joined a professional development course.

Answer the questions:

1. Why did you decide to take the course?
2. What has been useful so far?
3. Would you recommend it to a friend? Why or why not?

### Strong B2 answer model

I decided to take the course because I wanted to improve my communication skills at work. So far, the most useful part has been the practical activities, especially the role-plays, because they help me apply new vocabulary immediately. I would recommend the course to a friend who needs professional English, although I would warn them that they need to study between classes to get the full benefit.

### Why this answer works

- It answers all three questions.
- It gives reasons and examples.
- It uses linking words: because, so far, especially, although.
- It sounds natural and specific.

## Writing Task 2 - Opinion essay

Task 2 asks you to write a more developed response. You need a clear opinion, organized paragraphs, and examples.

### Opinion essay structure

| Paragraph | Job |
|---|---|
| Introduction | Paraphrase the topic and give your opinion. |
| Body 1 | First main reason + explanation + example. |
| Body 2 | Second main reason or contrast + explanation + example. |
| Conclusion | Summarize your position clearly. |

### B2 paragraph formula

Use this structure when you get stuck:

**Statement -> Reason -> Support**

Example:

**Statement:** Online courses can be effective for adults.  
**Reason:** They allow students to study around work and family responsibilities.  
**Support:** For example, a nurse who works night shifts can review recorded lessons on her days off.

## Writing self-checklist

| Check | Question to ask yourself |
|---|---|
| Task completion | Did I answer every part of the prompt? |
| Organization | Does each paragraph have one clear job? |
| Support | Did I explain my reasons with examples or details? |
| Grammar | Did I check verb tense, subject-verb agreement, articles, and plural forms? |
| Vocabulary | Did I use specific words instead of repeating basic words? |

# 5. Listening

The Listening section checks whether you understand spoken English in everyday, educational, professional, and public situations. In this mini mock, you will hear a conversation and a short talk or announcement. You answer multiple-choice questions about the main idea, details, speaker purpose, and implied meaning.

## Listening question types

| Type | What it asks | What to listen for | Example question |
|---|---|---|---|
| Main idea | The general topic or purpose. | Repeated idea, problem, or final decision. | What are the speakers mainly discussing? |
| Detail | Specific information. | Names, times, places, reasons, next steps. | What time will the meeting start? |
| Inference | What is suggested indirectly. | Tone, hesitation, contrast words, conclusions. | What does the woman imply about the schedule? |
| Speaker attitude | How a speaker feels. | Voice, word choice, reactions. | How does the man feel about the change? |
| Function | Why a speaker says something. | Requests, apologies, warnings, recommendations. | Why does the speaker mention the website? |

## Listening examples

### Example 1 - Detail

**Audio script:**  
Woman: I thought the training was today.  
Man: It was moved to Thursday because the instructor is sick.

**Question:** Why was the training moved?

A. The room was full  
B. The instructor is sick  
C. The man forgot  
D. It was canceled

**Answer:** B.  
**Why:** The man directly gives the reason: the instructor is sick.

### Example 2 - Function

**Audio script:**  
Speaker: Please arrive ten minutes early so we can begin the tour on time. Late arrivals may need to join the next group.

**Question:** What is the speaker mainly doing?

A. Giving instructions  
B. Complaining about visitors  
C. Advertising a job  
D. Describing a problem

**Answer:** A.  
**Why:** The speaker explains what visitors should do before the tour.

### Example 3 - Inference

**Audio script:**  
Woman: I can finish the report tonight, but I might need someone to check the numbers tomorrow.

**Question:** What does the woman imply?

A. She has already checked everything  
B. She may need help later  
C. She will not finish the report  
D. She does not understand the topic

**Answer:** B.  
**Why:** She says she might need someone to check the numbers tomorrow.

## How to take listening notes

| Do this | Example note | Why it helps |
|---|---|---|
| Write only key words | training moved Thu - instructor sick | You do not lose time writing full sentences. |
| Mark contrast words | but / however / actually | The answer often changes after contrast. |
| Listen for final decisions | decided: online meeting | Conversations often mention rejected options first. |
| Notice speaker attitude | worried / relieved / disappointed | Inference questions often depend on tone and reaction. |

> **Listening trap:** Do not choose the first word you recognize. MET-style options often repeat words from the audio, but the meaning may be different. Listen for the complete idea.

# 6. How to Review the Mini Mock After the Test

Correction is where the test becomes useful. Do not only count correct answers. Classify your mistakes so you know what to practice next.

## Mistake types

| Mistake type | Example | What it means | Next practice |
|---|---|---|---|
| Main idea | You chose a small detail as the answer. | You understood part of the text/audio but missed the overall purpose. | Practice title/purpose questions. |
| Detail | You missed a number, date, reason, or condition. | You need more careful scanning or listening for specific information. | Practice underlining evidence. |
| Inference | You chose an answer not directly supported. | You may be guessing from personal opinion. | Practice finding clues and explaining the conclusion. |
| Grammar | You chose the wrong tense or structure. | You need to connect form with meaning. | Review one grammar point and make five examples. |
| Vocabulary | You knew the word but not the collocation. | You need more natural phrase practice. | Build phrase banks, not single-word lists. |
| Writing organization | Your ideas were good but hard to follow. | You need clearer paragraph planning. | Use a 3-minute outline before writing. |

# 7. Teacher Use

- Before the mock: give students 5 minutes to read this guide and identify question types.
- During correction: ask students to justify answers with evidence, not only letter choices.
- After correction: choose one priority for homework, such as inference, tense control, or essay support.
- For speaking/writing-focused students: use the Writing checklist as a feedback rubric without giving a formal grade.

---

# 8. Student Next Steps

After the test, you should:

- review every incorrect answer and label the mistake type;
- rewrite two wrong Use of English items as correct sentences;
- write one improved paragraph from the Writing section;
- listen again to one audio script and write five key words before checking the answer key;
- choose one skill to focus on before the next mock.
