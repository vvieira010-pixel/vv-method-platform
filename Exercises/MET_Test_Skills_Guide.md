# MET Test Skills Guide

**Focus:** What the MET tests, what each section measures, and how students can prepare  
**Audience:** Student-facing and teacher-friendly  
**Date:** June 13, 2026

---

## 1. What is the MET?

MET stands for **Michigan English Test**. It is an English proficiency exam that measures how well a test taker can use English in real-world contexts.

The official 4-skill MET tests:

1. **Listening**
2. **Reading**
3. **Writing**
4. **Speaking**

Michigan Language Assessment also offers a 2-skill version for some institutional purposes, but healthcare, visa, university, and immigration-related goals commonly require the 4-skill version. Always check the requirement of the organization that will receive the score.

> **Important:** This guide explains the skills and preparation focus. It is not an official MET test booklet or official scoring document.

---

## 2. Official MET structure - quick overview

| Section | Official format summary | What it measures |
|---|---|---|
| Listening | Multiple-choice questions with one correct answer | Main idea, details, implied meaning |
| Reading | Grammar items plus reading comprehension | Grammar, main idea, details, inference, text purpose |
| Writing | Two writing tasks | Clear communication, organization, support, accuracy |
| Speaking | Five structured onscreen tasks | Real-world spoken communication |

# 3. Listening Skills

## What Listening measures

The MET Listening section assesses how well you understand spoken English in:

- personal situations;
- public situations;
- educational situations;
- professional situations.

The main skills are:

| Listening skill | What it means | Example question |
|---|---|---|
| Main idea | Understanding the general topic or purpose. | What are the speakers mainly discussing? |
| Detail | Identifying specific information. | What time does the event begin? |
| Implied meaning | Understanding what is suggested, not said directly. | What does the speaker imply about the new policy? |
| Speaker attitude | Understanding how someone feels. | How does the woman feel about the decision? |
| Function | Understanding why someone says something. | Why does the speaker mention the website? |

## What students usually find difficult

- The speaker says one option first, then changes the decision.
- The correct answer uses different words from the audio.
- The answer depends on tone or implication.
- Students try to write too much while listening.
- Students choose a word they recognize instead of the complete meaning.

## Listening strategy

1. Read the question before listening when possible.
2. Predict the type of answer: time, reason, opinion, problem, solution, attitude.
3. Take short notes with key words only.
4. Listen for contrast words: **but, however, actually, instead, although**.
5. After listening, choose the answer that matches the full idea.

## Listening note-taking model

| Audio information | Good note |
|---|---|
| The meeting was moved from Tuesday to Friday because the manager is traveling. | meeting Tues -> Fri / manager travel |
| Students must register online before Monday to receive the workbook. | reg online before Mon / workbook |
| The speaker is disappointed because few people completed the survey. | disappointed / few surveys |

# 4. Reading Skills

## What Reading measures

The MET Reading section includes both **grammar** and **reading comprehension**. Reading comprehension texts may include passages, emails, advertisements, articles, announcements, and related sets of texts.

The main reading skills are:

| Reading skill | What it means | Example question |
|---|---|---|
| Main idea | Understanding the overall message. | What is the passage mainly about? |
| Detail | Finding specific information. | According to the text, why was the program changed? |
| Inference | Understanding what is suggested indirectly. | What can be inferred about the writer? |
| Vocabulary in context | Understanding a word from surrounding clues. | The word "limited" is closest in meaning to... |
| Purpose | Understanding why the text was written. | Why did the writer send this email? |
| Text connection | Connecting ideas across related texts. | Which text gives a different opinion about the same issue? |

## What students usually find difficult

- They read too slowly and run out of time.
- They choose answers based on personal opinion.
- They confuse a detail with the main idea.
- They miss negative words like **not, except, unless, rarely**.
- They struggle with paraphrases.

## Reading strategy

1. Read the question first.
2. Identify the question type: main idea, detail, inference, vocabulary, purpose.
3. Find evidence in the text.
4. Compare the evidence with the options.
5. Eliminate answers that are too strong, not mentioned, opposite, or only partly true.

## Inference strategy

For inference questions, use this formula:

**Text clue + logical conclusion = inference**

Example:

- **Text clue:** The employee asked for more time even though she had finished most of the report.
- **Logical conclusion:** She probably wanted to check the report carefully.
- **Bad inference:** She did not do the report. This is too extreme and not supported.

# 5. Grammar / Use of English Skills

## What Grammar measures

The grammar part checks whether you can choose the correct word or phrase in context. It is not only about knowing rules. You need to understand how grammar changes meaning.

## Common B2 grammar areas

| Area | What to notice | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Verb tenses | Time and sequence | She has worked here since 2021. |
| Perfect forms | Connection between past and present or past before past | By the time we arrived, the lecture had started. |
| Conditionals | Real, possible, hypothetical, or past unreal situations | If I had more time, I would apply. |
| Passive voice | Focus on the action or result | The policy was updated last month. |
| Reported speech | Reporting what someone said | He said he had finished the form. |
| Modals | Ability, possibility, obligation, advice | You should review the instructions. |
| Relative clauses | Giving extra information | The course that I joined last month is useful. |
| Articles | General vs specific nouns | a hospital / the hospital / hospital care |
| Prepositions | Natural grammar after nouns, verbs, and adjectives | responsible for, interested in, depend on |

## Grammar strategy

Ask five quick questions:

1. What time is the sentence about?
2. Is the sentence active or passive?
3. Is the idea real, possible, hypothetical, or reported?
4. What comes before and after the blank?
5. Which option sounds natural and fits the meaning?

# 6. Vocabulary Skills

## What Vocabulary measures

Vocabulary questions check whether you can understand and use natural English in context. At B2, single words are not enough. You need phrases, collocations, word families, and topic vocabulary.

## Common vocabulary skill types

| Skill | What it means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Word meaning | Understanding a word in context | The service was reliable = dependable. |
| Word formation | Choosing the correct form | explain -> explanation -> explanatory |
| Collocation | Choosing natural word combinations | make a decision, take responsibility, strong evidence |
| Phrasal verbs | Understanding verb + particle combinations | fill out a form, put off a meeting |
| Academic/professional vocabulary | Understanding formal topics | policy, requirement, benefit, concern, evidence |
| Register | Choosing formal or informal language | I am writing to request... vs Can you send me...? |

## Vocabulary strategy

- Learn phrases, not isolated words.
- Write one example sentence for each new word.
- Group vocabulary by topic: work, health, education, community, technology, environment.
- Notice the grammar around the word: preposition, noun pattern, verb form.
- Review wrong answers and create a personal phrase bank.

# 7. Writing Skills

## Official Writing format

The official MET Writing section has two tasks:

1. Respond to three related questions about personal experiences.
2. Write a formal, multi-paragraph essay, typically 250+ words.

The official time is 45 minutes.

## What Writing measures

| Writing skill | What it means |
|---|---|
| Task completion | You answer everything the prompt asks. |
| Organization | Your ideas are arranged clearly in paragraphs. |
| Development | You explain your points with reasons, examples, and details. |
| Grammar accuracy | Your sentences are understandable and mostly accurate. |
| Vocabulary range | You use appropriate and specific words. |
| Coherence | Your ideas connect logically. |
| Register | Your tone fits the task, especially in formal writing. |

## Writing Task 1 strategy

For short response questions:

1. Answer each question directly.
2. Add a reason.
3. Add a small example or detail.
4. Use connectors naturally.
5. Do not write one-word or very general answers.

### Task 1 mini model

**Question:** Why did you choose your current profession?

**B2 answer:**  
I chose nursing because I wanted a career that would allow me to help people directly. It can be stressful, especially during long shifts, but I enjoy seeing patients recover and feel more confident.

## Writing Task 2 strategy

For essays, use this structure:

| Paragraph | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Introduction | Present the topic and your opinion. |
| Body paragraph 1 | First reason + explanation + example. |
| Body paragraph 2 | Second reason or balanced point + explanation + example. |
| Conclusion | Restate your position clearly. |

## Useful B2 writing connectors

| Function | Useful phrases |
|---|---|
| Add ideas | In addition, Furthermore, Another important point is... |
| Give reasons | This is because..., The main reason is... |
| Give examples | For example, For instance, In my experience... |
| Contrast | However, On the other hand, Although this may be true... |
| Conclude | Overall, In conclusion, For these reasons... |

# 8. Speaking Skills

## Official Speaking format

The MET Speaking section is a structured onscreen speaking test with five tasks. It takes about 10 minutes. Responses are recorded digitally and scored by trained raters.

## The five Speaking tasks

| Task | What you do | Main skill |
|---|---|---|
| Task 1 | Describe a picture. | Description, observation, basic inference |
| Task 2 | Share a related personal experience. | Narration, past tenses, organization |
| Task 3 | Give an opinion. | Opinion, reasons, support |
| Task 4 | Discuss pros and cons of a situation. | Balanced discussion, comparison |
| Task 5 | Provide convincing arguments to a person of authority. | Persuasion, problem-solving, formal tone |

## Speaking strategy by task

### Task 1 - Picture description

Use this order:

1. General situation: who, where, what.
2. Key details: actions, objects, relationship.
3. Inference: what may be happening and why.

Useful phrases:

- In the picture, I can see...
- It looks like...
- They seem to be...
- This might be happening because...

### Task 2 - Personal experience

Use a simple story structure:

1. Situation
2. Problem or main event
3. Action
4. Result
5. Reflection

Useful phrases:

- A few years ago...
- At first, I felt...
- What I did was...
- In the end...
- This experience taught me...

### Task 3 - Opinion

Use this structure:

**Opinion -> Reason 1 -> Example -> Reason 2 -> Conclusion**

Useful phrases:

- In my opinion...
- The main reason is that...
- For example...
- Another point is...
- That is why I believe...

### Task 4 - Advantages and disadvantages

Use this structure:

1. Introduce both sides.
2. Advantage 1 + support.
3. Disadvantage 1 + support.
4. Final balanced conclusion.

Useful phrases:

- One advantage is...
- On the other hand...
- A possible drawback is...
- Overall, I think...

### Task 5 - Persuasion

Use this structure:

1. Address the authority respectfully.
2. State the problem.
3. Give strong reasons.
4. Offer a practical solution.
5. End with a clear request.

Useful phrases:

- I understand your concern, but...
- I would like to suggest...
- This change would benefit...
- One possible solution is...
- For these reasons, I hope you will consider...

# 9. B2 Skill Profile

A strong B2 student can usually:

| Skill | B2 student can... |
|---|---|
| Listening | Follow clear speech on familiar topics and identify important details. |
| Reading | Understand articles, notices, emails, and arguments on common topics. |
| Grammar | Use a range of tenses, modals, passive forms, and conditionals with reasonable control. |
| Vocabulary | Use topic vocabulary and common collocations naturally. |
| Writing | Write organized paragraphs with reasons and examples. |
| Speaking | Speak for 60-90 seconds with clear organization and enough detail. |

A student moving from B1 to B2 often needs more work on:

- inference;
- deeper support and examples;
- tense consistency;
- collocations;
- formal tone;
- listening to longer speech;
- organizing answers under time pressure.

# 10. Study Priorities for MET Preparation

## Priority 1 - Inference

Inference appears in both Reading and Listening. Students must learn to make logical conclusions from evidence.

Practice:

- Read a short text and write: **What does it say? What does it suggest?**
- Listen to a short dialogue and identify the speaker's attitude.
- Explain why wrong inference options are too strong or unsupported.

## Priority 2 - Timed speaking organization

Students should practice giving clear answers within time limits.

Practice:

- 60 seconds for picture description and personal experience.
- 90 seconds for opinion, pros/cons, and persuasion.
- Record and review one answer per day.

## Priority 3 - Grammar in context

Students should not study grammar only as isolated rules. They need sentence-level and paragraph-level practice.

Practice:

- Correct short texts.
- Rewrite sentences using passive voice, reported speech, and conditionals.
- Identify the reason for each grammar choice.

## Priority 4 - Writing support

B2 writing needs reasons, examples, and clear paragraphing.

Practice:

- Plan before writing.
- Use Statement -> Reason -> Support.
- Rewrite weak paragraphs with stronger examples.

## Priority 5 - Vocabulary by topic

MET topics are connected to real-world situations, so students need flexible topic vocabulary.

Useful topic areas:

- health and healthcare;
- work and professional life;
- education and training;
- community and public services;
- technology;
- environment;
- family and personal decisions;
- transportation and city life.

# 11. Teacher Notes: How to Use This Guide in Class

## Before a mock test

- Review the section overview.
- Show students the question types.
- Do 2 or 3 examples together.
- Remind students that the goal is diagnosis, not only a score.

## During correction

Ask students to explain:

- Where is the evidence?
- What type of question is it?
- Why is the correct answer better?
- Why are the other options wrong?

## After correction

Choose one priority only, such as:

- inference;
- listening details;
- tense control;
- essay organization;
- speaking support;
- collocations.

Then assign focused homework connected to that priority.
