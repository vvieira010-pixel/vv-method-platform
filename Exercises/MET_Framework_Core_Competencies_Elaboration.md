# MET Framework Core Competencies Elaboration

> **Note:** This document is a pedagogical elaboration of the four fundamental competencies that comprise the Michigan English Test (MET) framework. It layers over the official MET scoring scales defined in the platform's diagnostic system (`prompts.js`) and complements the student-facing `MET_Test_Skills_Guide.md`. The structural details, strategies, and criteria presented herein are derived directly from the MET_Test_Skills_Guide.md.

As an English proficiency standard designed to measure real-world communicative ability, the MET paradigm relies on the collective mastery of four core language competencies: Listening, Reading (including integrated lexical and grammatical systems), Writing, and Speaking.

---

## 1. Competency I: Listening Skills

The Listening competency within the MET framework measures a candidate's ability to process and comprehend spoken English across diverse communicative contexts. It requires test-takers to decode acoustic input and retrieve both explicit and implicit information across four primary settings: personal, public, educational, and professional.

### Core Sub-Skills and Dimensions

To demonstrate mastery in Listening, candidates must execute five critical cognitive operations:

* **Main Idea Comprehension:** Discerning the overall topic, general theme, or overarching communicative purpose of a spoken interaction.
* **Detail Identification:** Locating and extracting specific factual points or concrete pieces of information from the discourse.
* **Implied Meaning (Inference):** Extrapolating underlying messages or suggestions that are not explicitly stated by the speakers.
* **Speaker Attitude Analysis:** Decoding vocal cues, tone, and register to identify how a speaker feels or their emotional stance toward a topic or decision.
* **Functional Understanding:** Determining the precise pragmatic reason behind a speaker's utterance (i.e., understanding *why* someone says what they say).

### Critical Obstacles and Strategic Interventions

Listening performance is frequently compromised by specific systemic challenges. The framework addresses these through targeted behavioral strategies:

1. **Mid-Stream Information Shifts:** Speakers often state an initial option or decision and subsequently alter it.
   * *Strategy:* Candidates must avoid rushing to conclusions. They must listen to the entirety of the exchange and trace the logical flow of the conversation before selecting an option.

2. **Lexical Distractors:** Incorrect response options often reuse exact words from the audio stream to mislead candidates.
   * *Strategy:* Candidates must prioritize semantic equivalence over verbatim matching, focusing on paraphrased options that capture the complete idea.

3. **Note-Taking Overload:** Attempting to transcribe spoken passages word-for-word leads to cognitive overload and missed details.
   * *Strategy:* Implement a lean, symbolic note-taking model focused on abbreviations and logical relationships (e.g., representing "The meeting was moved from Tuesday to Friday because the manager is traveling" as `meeting Tues -> Fri / manager travel`).

4. **Discourse Signposts:** Changes in directional flow are marked by transitional cues.
   * *Strategy:* Active monitoring must focus heavily on contrast and concession markers, specifically: **but, however, actually, instead,** and **although**.

## 2. Competency II: Reading Skills (with Integrated Grammar and Vocabulary)

The Reading competency evaluates a candidate's capacity to comprehend written texts of varying genres and complexities, including single and connected passages, emails, advertisements, articles, and announcements. Within the MET framework, this competency operates in tandem with underlying structural (Grammar) and lexical (Vocabulary) sub-systems.

### Core Reading Sub-Skills

* **Main Idea:** Determining the primary thesis or collective message of a written text.
* **Detail:** Finding specific, localized evidence within the text to answer direct factual inquiries.
* **Inference:** Using textual clues to arrive at logical conclusions that are not explicitly spelled out. The structural formula for executing this process is:

  > **Text clue + Logical conclusion = Inference**

* **Vocabulary in Context:** Discerning the precise meaning of a word or phrase based on the semantic clues surrounding it.
* **Purpose:** Identifying the author's underlying motivation or goal in writing the text.
* **Text Connection:** Evaluating and linking ideas, arguments, or perspectives across multiple related texts.

### Integrated Sub-Systems: Grammar and Vocabulary

A candidate's reading comprehension and language processing are supported by two structural pillars:

#### A. Structural Control (Grammar in Context)

Candidates must understand how grammatical choices alter meaning within sentences and paragraphs, focusing on:

* **Verb Tenses & Perfect Forms:** Establishing time, sequence, and connections between past and present (e.g., past before past).
* **Conditionals:** Managing real, hypothetical, or past unreal situations.
* **Passive Voice & Reported Speech:** Shifting focus to actions or results, and reporting previous statements accurately.
* **Modals, Relative Clauses, Articles, & Prepositions:** Demonstrating natural sentence-level structure and functional control.

#### B. Lexical Control (Vocabulary in Context)

At the target B2 proficiency level, vocabulary must extend beyond single words to encompass multi-word items, collocations, and register-appropriate styling:

* **Collocations:** Utilizing natural word pairings (e.g., *make a decision*, *take responsibility*, *strong evidence*).
* **Phrasal Verbs & Word Formation:** Navigating verb-particle combinations and shifting word forms (e.g., *explain* → *explanation* → *explanatory*).
* **Register:** Choosing language appropriate to the setting (e.g., distinguishing between formal academic/professional vocabulary and informal phrasing).

## 3. Competency III: Writing Skills

The Writing competency assesses a candidate's ability to communicate ideas clearly, accurately, and persuasively in written English. This competency is evaluated through two distinct communicative tasks that require varying linguistic strategies and organizational structures.

### Structural Requirements of Writing Tasks

1. **Task 1 (Personal Experience Responses):** Candidates respond to three related prompts regarding personal experiences. Mastery requires:
   * Answering each question directly without reverting to single-word or overly generalized responses.
   * Elaborating on each answer with a distinct reason and a concrete personal detail or example.
   * Utilizing transitions naturally to link ideas.

2. **Task 2 (Formal Multi-Paragraph Essay):** Candidates produce a formal academic or professional essay of 250+ words. The structural execution must follow a rigid four-part paragraph model:
   * **Introduction:** Introducing the topic clearly and stating the writer's thesis/opinion.
   * **Body Paragraph 1:** Presenting the first supporting reason, complete with explanation and evidence.
   * **Body Paragraph 2:** Presenting a second supporting reason or a balanced counter-perspective, complete with explanation and evidence.
   * **Conclusion:** Restating the position clearly and synthesizing the arguments.

### Key Assessment Standards

The MET framework scores written output based on seven defined qualitative criteria:

| Standard | Description |
|---|---|
| Task Completion | Addressing every component of the prompt fully. |
| Organization | Structuring ideas logically across clear paragraph boundaries. |
| Development | Providing adequate depth through reasoning, examples, and details. |
| Grammar Accuracy | Maintaining control over sentence structures so that meaning is clear and errors are minimized. |
| Vocabulary Range | Deploying precise, varied, and topic-appropriate terminology. |
| Coherence | Connecting sentences and ideas in a logical, readable flow using B2 connectors. |
| Register | Matching the tone of the written response to the formal requirements of the task. |

**B2 Connectors:** In addition, Furthermore, This is because, For instance, However, Overall, In conclusion.

## 4. Competency IV: Speaking Skills

The Speaking competency measures real-world oral communication through five structured, onscreen tasks. It evaluates a candidate's ability to organize spoken language under time pressure, maintain structural accuracy, and execute specific pragmatic functions.

### Analytical Breakdown of Speaking Tasks

* **Task 1: Picture Description**
  * Focus: Description, systematic observation, and basic inference.
  * Execution Formula: Establish the general situation (who, where, what) → move to key supporting details (actions, objects, relationships) → conclude with logical inferences about what is happening and why.

* **Task 2: Personal Experience Narration**
  * Focus: Narrative sequencing, past tense control, and chronological organization.
  * Execution Formula: Guide the listener through five stages: Situation → Problem/Main Event → Action Taken → Result → Reflection/Lesson.

* **Task 3: Opinion Presentation**
  * Focus: Direct stance-taking and logical argumentation.
  * Execution Formula: Opinion → Reason 1 → Example → Reason 2 → Conclusion.

* **Task 4: Advantages and Disadvantages (Comparison)**
  * Focus: Balanced discussion and comparison of counterpoints.
  * Execution Formula: Introduce both sides → present one major advantage with support → contrast with one major drawback with support → deliver a balanced concluding synthesis.

* **Task 5: Persuasion**
  * Focus: Persuasive argumentation, problem-solving, and register control.
  * Execution Formula: Respectfully address an authority figure → state the core problem → present strong supporting reasons → offer a highly practical solution → close with a direct, formal request.

## 5. Summary of Target B2 Competency Profile

To successfully master the MET framework, a candidate must reach a consistent B2 profile across all four competencies. This entails:

* Following spoken discourse on familiar topics while extracting implied meaning.
* Reading and connecting arguments across disparate, complex texts.
* Writing highly organized paragraphs using cohesive transition markers and explicit supporting evidence.
* Speaking with sustained organization for 60 to 90 seconds while managing complex structures like tenses, modals, and conditionals under timed conditions.
