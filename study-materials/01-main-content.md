## 1. MET Speaking (Competency IV)

### Overview

5 structured speaking tasks (Q1–Q5) under timed conditions. Each task tests a different communicative purpose with a specific register requirement.

### Q1 — Describe a Picture (60 seconds)

**Purpose**: Describe a scene with full spatial coverage and precise vocabulary.

**Execution Formula**: General situation → key details → logical inferences

**Stages**:
1. **General situation** — who is in the picture, where they are, what is generally happening
2. **Key details** — actions, objects, relationships; cover foreground, background, centre, left, right
3. **Logical inferences** — what the people seem to be doing, what is likely happening next

**Sentence Frames**:
- "This picture shows…"
- "In the foreground, I can see…"
- "In the background, there is/are…"
- "The people seem to be…"
- "It looks like…"

**Self-Check**:
- Started with a general description (not just a list)
- Described people AND actions, not only objects
- Covered foreground/background/spatial details
- Ended with an inference or interpretation

**Common Trap**: Only listing objects without explaining the situation. Avoid: "I see a table, a chair, a window, and a person." Better: "This picture shows an office worker sitting at a desk. In the background, there is a window with a view of the city."

**Assessment Criteria**:
- Task Completion: relevance, quantity, elaboration
- Language Resources: vocabulary range, grammar control
- Intelligibility: pronunciation, fluency, clarity

### Q2 — Personal Experience (60 seconds)

**Purpose**: Narrate a specific personal experience with a clear sequence of events.

**Execution Formula**: Situation → Problem/Main Event → Action Taken → Result → Reflection/Lesson

**Stages**:
1. **Situation** — when it happened, where, who was involved
2. **Problem/Main Event** — what occurred, the central incident
3. **Action Taken** — what you or others did in response
4. **Result** — how the situation ended
5. **Reflection/Lesson** — what you learned or how you felt

**Sentence Frames**:
- "I remember a time when…"
- "This happened when I was…"
- "At first…"
- "After that…"
- "In the end…"
- "I felt… because…"

**Self-Check**:
- Specific experience, not a general habit
- Past tense consistent throughout
- Clear setup → event → outcome arc
- Used narrative connectors (first, then, after that, finally)

**Common Trap**: Speaking in general terms instead of telling one specific story. Avoid: "I usually help people at work." Better: "I remember one night shift when a patient needed urgent assistance…"

### Q3 — Personal Opinion (60 seconds)

**Purpose**: Express and defend a clear personal opinion with reasons.

**Execution Formula**: Opinion → Reason 1 → Example → Reason 2 → Conclusion

**Stages**:
1. **Opinion** — state your position clearly in the first sentence
2. **Reason 1** — your first supporting reason
3. **Example** — a specific example or elaboration
4. **Reason 2** — a second supporting reason
5. **Conclusion** — restate or reinforce your position

**Sentence Frames**:
- "In my opinion…"
- "I believe that…"
- "The main reason is…"
- "Another reason is…"
- "For example…"
- "That is why I think…"

**Self-Check**:
- ONE clear opinion (not both sides)
- Two reasons, or one reason + one example
- Informal but committed language ("I believe…")
- NOT balanced — this is one-sided

**Common Trap**: Giving both sides weakens commitment. For Q3, do NOT discuss pros and cons — pick one position and defend it.

### Q4 — Advantages & Disadvantages (90 seconds)

**Purpose**: Present a balanced analysis of both benefits and drawbacks.

**Execution Formula**: Introduce both sides → advantage with support → drawback with support → balanced concluding synthesis

**Stages**:
1. **Introduce both sides** — state that there are advantages and disadvantages
2. **Present one major advantage** with supporting detail
3. **Contrast with one major drawback** with supporting detail
4. **Balanced concluding synthesis** — "on balance" statement

**Sentence Frames**:
- "There are several advantages to this."
- "One benefit is…"
- "However, there are also disadvantages."
- "One problem is…"
- "On balance, I think…"

**Self-Check**:
- BOTH sides covered (covering only one side loses ~1/3 of marks)
- Roughly equal time per side (~40 seconds each)
- NEUTRAL register (not giving personal opinion)
- Clear transition between sides

**Common Trap**: Covering only one side, or giving a personal opinion instead of balanced analysis.

### Q5 — Persuade an Authority (90 seconds)

**Purpose**: Make a formal, persuasive argument to an authority figure.

**Execution Formula**: Address authority → State problem → Present reasons → Offer practical solution → Formal request

**Stages**:
1. **Address authority figure respectfully**
2. **State core problem clearly**
3. **Present strong supporting reasons**
4. **Offer highly practical solution**
5. **Close with formal request**

**Sentence Frames**:
- "I strongly believe that…"
- "I would recommend…"
- "This would be beneficial because…"
- "One important reason is…"
- "I understand there may be concerns, but…"
- "For these reasons, I hope you will consider this."

**Self-Check**:
- Position/recommendation in FIRST 10–15 seconds
- FORMAL register throughout (not "I think" → "I strongly believe")
- Address authority directly
- Maintained commitment, no hedging

**Common Trap**: Sounding informal (using "maybe" or "I sort of think"), not committing, or presenting both sides. Q5 is one-sided persuasion.

### Speaking Rating Scale (0–4 per category)

| Score | Task Completion | Language Resources | Intelligibility |
|---|---|---|---|
| 4 | Fully relevant, extensive detail | Complex grammar, broad vocab, rare errors | Smooth, clear, minimal hesitation |
| 3 | Relevant, general detail | Some complex structures, appropriate vocab | Some hesitation, generally clear |
| 2 | Mostly relevant, some difficulty | Simple patterns controlled, noticeable errors | Sometimes hesitant, some listener effort |
| 1 | Short/simple, difficulty completing | Simple sentences, basic errors | Frequent pauses, significant effort |
| 0 | No response or irrelevant | Insufficient language | Not comprehensible |

### Q4 vs Q5 — Critical Distinction

| Aspect | Q4 (Adv/Disadv) | Q5 (Persuade Authority) |
|---|---|---|
| Register | Neutral, balanced | Formal, committed |
| Personal opinion | No | Yes |
| Sides | Both required | One side only |
| Opener | "There are several benefits and drawbacks…" | "I strongly believe…" / "As far as I'm concerned…" |
| Time | 90s | 90s |

## 2. MET Writing (Competency III)

### Overview

Two sections: W1 (3 short questions) and W2 (1 extended essay). Assessed on 5 criteria each scored 0–4.

### W1Q1 — Short Answer (Question 1)

**Structure**: Direct answer → detail → small explanation

**Response Model**: Direct answer + reason + concrete personal detail

**Self-Check**:
- Answered directly in first sentence
- At least one specific detail or example
- Expanded beyond one short sentence
- Appropriate tense (usually past simple or present simple)

**Common Trap**: Giving only one short sentence with no development.

### W1Q2 — Short Answer (Question 2)

**Structure**: Answer → reason → support

**Response Model**: Direct answer + reason + concrete personal detail

**Frames**: "Because…", "Since…", "For example…", "However…", "I prefer…", "In my opinion…"

**Self-Check**:
- Clear reason (not repeating Q1 answer)
- Supported with example or explanation
- Used connectors
- Did NOT simply repeat ideas from Q1

**Common Trap**: Repeating the same idea from Q1.

### W1Q3 — Short Answer (Question 3)

**Structure**: Position/idea → explanation → example or consequence

**Response Model**: Direct answer + reason + concrete personal detail

**Frames**: "Should…", "Would…", "Could…", "Might…", "On the other hand…", "As a result…"

**Self-Check**:
- Extended the topic (advice, comparison, future consequence)
- Appropriate modal verbs
- Organized ideas clearly
- Answered the EXACT question

**Common Trap**: Ending too quickly or not answering the exact question.

### W2 — Formal Essay

**Structure**: Introduction → Body × 2 → Conclusion

**Assessment Standards**:
1. Task Completion
2. Organization
3. Development
4. Grammar Accuracy
5. Vocabulary Range
6. Coherence
7. Register

**B2 Connectors for Writing**:
- In addition, Furthermore — adding points
- This is because — explaining
- For instance — giving examples
- However — contrasting
- Overall, In conclusion — summarizing

**Self-Check**:
- Clear position in introduction
- Each body paragraph = one main reason + explanation + example
- Formal or semi-formal language
- Conclusion restates main idea + final comment
- At least 250 words, multiple paragraphs

**Common Trap**: One long paragraph, opinions without support, or informal spoken language.

### Writing Rating Scale (0–4 per category)

| Score | Grammatical Accuracy | Vocabulary | Mechanics | Cohesion/Org | Task Completion |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 4 | Rare errors, advanced grammar | Sophisticated, properly used | No boundary errors, almost no spelling errors | Very cohesive, always successful | Fully relevant, detail developed |
| 3 | Simple error-free, complex may err | Simple + sophisticated mix | Minor punctuation/spelling errors | Generally cohesive | Relevant, adequate detail |
| 2 | Frequent errors, some obscure meaning | Mostly simple, some misuse | Frequent errors | Partially cohesive | Mainly relevant, minimal detail |
| 1 | Pervasive errors, reader guesses | Only simple words, failed attempts | Little/no control, pervasive spelling | Not cohesive | Very short, partially relevant |
| 0 | Impossible to process | No relevant vocabulary | No legible text | No clear ideas | No response |

## 3. MET Reading + Grammar + Vocabulary (Competency II)

### Reading

**Passage Types**:
- Single texts — multiple choice (articles, blogs, information texts)
- Multiple texts — multiple choice (compare/contrast across sources)
- True/False/Not Given — detail recognition and inference

**Skills Tested**:
- main_idea — identifying the gist
- detail — locating specific information
- inference — reading between the lines
- vocabulary_in_context — understanding words from context
- reference — tracking pronoun and word references
- purpose_tone — identifying why the text was written
- text_organization — following argument structure
- paraphrase_recognition — matching rephrased ideas
- distractor_recognition — avoiding tempting wrong answers
- scanning — finding specific information quickly
- skimming — getting the general idea
- time_management — pacing across sections

**Common Failure Modes**:
- main_idea: choosing a supporting detail instead of the gist
- distractor_recognition: choosing the option with familiar words from the text
- inference: over-literal reading, not making logical connections
- scanning: re-reading instead of targeting specific information

**Reading Inference Formula**: Text clue + Logical conclusion = Inference

### Grammar Sub-System

Grammar is tested within the MET Reading section. Core areas:

1. **Verb Tenses & Perfect Forms** — Present Simple, Present Continuous, Past Simple, Past Continuous, Present Perfect, Past Perfect, Future forms
2. **Conditionals** — Zero, First (real future), Second (unreal present), Third (unreal past)
3. **Passive Voice & Reported Speech** — be + past participle; tense backshift
4. **Modals** — Must/mustn't (obligation/prohibition), Should (advice), Can/could (ability), May/might (possibility)
5. **Relative Clauses** — Defining (who, which, that) and non-defining
6. **Articles** — A/an (indefinite), The (definite), Zero article
7. **Prepositions** — Time, place, movement, dependent prepositions

**Grammar Module Types** (from B2 bank):
- Sentence Completion (MCQ)
- Open Cloze (passage fill)
- Error Correction (fix sentences)
- Word Formation (blank)
- Mixed Tenses (blank)
- Conditionals & Wishes (blank)
- Modals (blank)

### Vocabulary Sub-System

Vocabulary is tested within the MET Reading section. Core areas:

1. **Collocations** — words that naturally go together (make a reservation, set boundaries, keep a routine)
2. **Phrasal Verbs & Word Formation** — verb + particle combinations; prefixes and suffixes
3. **Register** — formal vs. informal vocabulary choice

**Vocabulary Sub-Skills**:
- general_met_vocabulary
- healthcare_vocabulary (for nursing/healthcare candidates)
- academic_vocabulary
- topic_vocabulary
- word_choice
- collocation
- paraphrasing
- inference_language
- connector_phrases

**Vocabulary Modules** (12 modules, 20 target words each):
Health & Lifestyle, Workplace Communication, Education & Learning, Technology, Community & Transportation, Food & Family, Environment, General Science, Customer Service, Travel & Culture, Speaking — Picture Description, Science & Research

## 4. MET Listening (Competency I)

### Overview

Three parts with increasing length and complexity.

### Part 1: Short Conversations

- 10 items
- Each: short dialogue + 1 question
- Tests main idea, detail, speaker purpose

### Part 2: Longer Conversations

- 5 items
- Each: extended dialogue + 4 questions
- Tests main idea, detail, inference, topic shifts

### Part 3: Short Talks

- 2 items
- Each: monologue/announcement + 4 questions
- Tests main idea, detail, inference, purpose/attitude

### Skills Tested

- main_idea — understanding the overall point
- detail — catching specific information
- inference — understanding implied meaning
- speaker_purpose — identifying why someone is speaking
- vocabulary_in_context — understanding words from audio context
- prediction_anticipation — predicting what comes next
- distractor_resistance — avoiding tempting wrong answers
- listening_under_time_pressure — maintaining focus

### Listening Sections Breakdown

- **Section 1**: Short dialogues — identify main point and intent
- **Section 2**: Extended dialogues — track topic shifts and speaker roles
- **Section 3**: Lectures/monologues — main idea, detail, inference
- **Section 4**: Academic discussions — complex exchanges, identify roles and views

### Strategic Interventions

1. **Mid-Stream Information Shifts**: Speakers often state an initial option then change it. Listen to the entire exchange before concluding.

2. **Lexical Distractors**: Wrong options reuse exact words from the audio. Prioritize semantic equivalence over verbatim matching.

3. **Note-Taking Overload**: Full transcription causes cognitive overload. Use lean symbolic notes: abbreviations + logical relationships (e.g., "meeting Tues → Fri / manager travel").

4. **Discourse Signposts**: Real answers often follow contrast markers: but, however, actually, instead, although.

## 5. Test Strategy (Cross-Cutting)

### Skills

- time_management
- question_type_recognition
- distractor_management
- evidence_selection
- self_correction
- exam_anxiety_control

### Speaking-Specific Strategies

- Use starter phrases immediately (don't wait for a perfect sentence)
- Structure: Opinion → Reason → Support
- For Q4: split time roughly 40s per side
- For Q5: state position in first 10–15 seconds

### Writing-Specific Strategies

- Use the expansion frame: "Because… For example… This shows that…"
- Check: did I answer the exact question?
- At least 250 words for W2, multiple paragraphs
- Use B2 connectors (In addition, Furthermore, However, Overall)

### Reading/Listening-Specific Strategies

- Read/listen for gist first, then details
- Watch for lexical distractors (familiar words ≠ correct answer)
- Use the inference formula: Text clue + Logical conclusion = Inference
- Listen for contrast markers (but, however, actually)
