# MET Exam — Discussion Questions for Deeper Learning

## Speaking (Competency IV)

### Q1 — Picture Description
1. What is the difference between listing objects and describing a scene? Write a 2-sentence description of your current room — first as a list, then as a description. What changed?
2. Why is it important to include an inference (what the people seem to be doing) at the end? What happens if you only describe what you see?
3. Think of a picture you could describe. What spatial words would you use for foreground, background, left, right, centre?

### Q2 — Personal Experience
4. Tell a story about a time you helped someone. First, tell it in 3 sentences using only Past Simple. Then expand it using the Situation → Problem → Action → Result → Reflection structure. What did you add?
5. Why is it important to tell ONE specific experience instead of describing a general habit? How does the examiner know the difference?
6. Practice past tense consistency. Tell a 30-second story, then write it down. Circle every verb. Are they all in the same tense?

### Q3 — Personal Opinion
7. Take a position: "Should cities ban cars from downtown areas?" Give ONE clear opinion with TWO reasons. Why is it better to commit to one side than to give both?
8. What is the difference between "I think" and "I believe"? Which sounds more committed? Why does commitment matter for Q3?
9. Practice the Opinion → Reason → Example → Reason → Conclusion structure on a topic of your choice. Time yourself: can you do it in 60 seconds?

### Q4 — Advantages & Disadvantages
10. Choose a topic (e.g., "working from home"). List 2 advantages and 2 disadvantages. Now speak for 90 seconds covering both sides. Did you spend roughly equal time on each side?
11. Why is neutral register important for Q4? What happens if you say "I love working from home" instead of "One advantage of working from home is..."
12. Practice the transition pivot: "However, there are also some disadvantages." Write 3 different transition sentences you could use.

### Q5 — Persuade an Authority
13. Imagine you need to persuade your manager to implement flexible hours. Write your opening 2 sentences — state your position within the first 10-15 seconds.
14. Compare an informal and a formal version of the same request: "I think we should..." vs. "I strongly believe that we should implement..." What makes the second version more persuasive to an authority?
15. Why should you NOT present both sides in Q5? How is Q5 different from Q4?

## Writing (Competency III)

16. Take the sentence "I agree because it is important." Expand it using: For example… This shows that… What does the expansion add that was missing?
17. Write 2 versions of a W1 answer to "What is your favorite free-time activity?" Version 1: one short sentence. Version 2: answer + reason + specific detail. Compare them. Which would score higher?
18. For W2 (essay), why does each body paragraph need ONE main reason + explanation + example? What happens if you put multiple reasons in one paragraph?
19. Write an outline for a W2 essay on "Should schools require students to wear uniforms?" Use: Introduction → Body 1 (for) → Body 2 (against) → Conclusion. Fill in the main point for each section.
20. Read a sample essay and identify: Task Completion, Organization, Development, Grammar Accuracy, Vocabulary Range, Coherence, Register. Which standard needs the most improvement?

## Grammar

21. Explain the difference between First Conditional and Second Conditional in your own words. Create one example sentence for each.
22. When would you use "who" vs. "which" in a relative clause? Create 2 example sentences.
23. Correct this sentence: "If I will have more time, I would travel more." What rule was broken? How would you explain it to a classmate?
24. Why is Present Perfect used with "for" and "since" but not with specific past time expressions (yesterday, last week)?
25. Write 3 rules for using articles (a, an, the, zero article) with singular countable nouns.

## Vocabulary

26. Replace "important" with a more specific word in these sentences: (a) Exercise is important. (b) Communication is important in nursing. (c) This policy is important for the community.
27. Create a word bank of 10 B2-level alternatives for common words: good, bad, happy, sad, big, small, thing, get, do, make.
28. What is a collocation? Find 3 collocations from the practice units (e.g., "make a reservation," "set boundaries"). Why do collocations matter at B2 level?
29. Choose a topic (health, technology, environment). List 10 topic-specific words at B1 level and upgrade each to a B2 alternative.

## Listening (Competency I)

30. Listen to a short audio dialogue. What was the first option the speaker mentioned? Did they change their mind? Why does this matter for MET Listening?
31. Create a lexical distractor: write a question about a short audio where one answer option reuses exact words from the audio but is wrong. Why is this trap effective?
32. Practice note-taking: listen to a 30-second announcement and take notes using only symbols and abbreviations. Then answer 2 detail questions. Did the notes help or distract?
33. Identify 5 discourse signposts (but, however, actually, instead, although) in a listening script. What does each one signal about what follows?

## Reading (Competency II)

34. Read a short passage. What is the main idea? Now find a sentence that States a supporting detail. What is the difference between the main idea and the detail?
35. Practice the inference formula: Text clue + Logical conclusion = Inference. Find a text clue in a passage, add a logical conclusion, and state the inference.
36. Why do examiners use paraphrases in reading questions? How can you practice paraphrase recognition?
37. Read a passage and identify: one lexical distractor (an answer option with familiar words but wrong meaning) and explain why it's tempting.

## Test Strategy (Cross-Cutting)

38. You have 45 minutes for Reading. How would you allocate time across 3 passages? What is your strategy if you get stuck on one question?
39. During a speaking test, you forget the word you wanted to use. What 2 strategies can you use to keep going without freezing?
40. After writing an essay, list 3 things you should check before submitting. Why is self-correction an important test-taking skill?
41. How do you manage anxiety during a timed speaking task? What can you do in the first 5 seconds to calm down and start strong?

## MET Exam Structure

42. Draw the relationship between the 4 competencies and the 7 tracked skills. Which competency covers grammar? Why is grammar not a separate competency?
43. What overall score do you need for B2? What does "overall ≥58, speaking ≥59" mean for your study priorities?
44. If your target is B2, which CEFR level descriptors should you focus on? What changes between B1 and B2?
45. Explain the diagnosis cycle in your own words. Why is the cycle better than one-off tests?

## Self-Assessment

46. Rate yourself on each of the 7 skills (Speaking, Writing, Reading, Listening, Grammar, Vocabulary, Test Strategy) as: Secure, Consolidating, Developing, or Not Yet Evident. What evidence supports each rating?
47. Which gap type do you most commonly have — conceptual, procedural, or communication? Give a specific example from your own learning.
48. Set a SMART goal for your next study session: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound.
49. After completing a practice task, do a confidence calibration check: rate your confidence before seeing the answer, then compare with actual performance. Are you overconfident or underconfident?
50. What is the single most important thing you learned from these study materials? Write it in one sentence. Then explain why it matters for your MET preparation.
