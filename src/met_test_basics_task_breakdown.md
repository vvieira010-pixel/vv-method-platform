# MET Test Basics: Task-by-Task Breakdown

**Purpose:** Quick reference for teachers, students, and platform content design.  
**Exam:** Michigan English Test (MET), 4-skill version  
**Skills:** Writing, Listening, Reading, Speaking  
**Total time:** About 155 minutes  
**Important note:** Exact prompts change from test to test. This guide explains what each section normally asks students to do and what teachers should train.

---

## 1. Quick Overview

| Section | Time | Parts / Tasks | Main Format | Scoring |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|
| Writing | 45 minutes | 2 tasks | Short responses + essay | Human raters |
| Listening | 35 minutes | 3 parts, 50 questions | Multiple choice | Computer-scored |
| Reading | 65 minutes | 3 parts, 50 questions | Grammar + reading multiple choice | Computer-scored |
| Speaking | 10 minutes | 5 tasks | Recorded spoken responses | Human raters |

---

## 2. Writing Section

### General format

The MET Writing section has **2 tasks** and lasts **45 minutes**.

| Task | What the student sees | What the student must produce |
|---|---|---|
| Task 1 | Three related questions on one theme | Short connected responses to each question |
| Task 2 | One essay prompt | A formal multi-paragraph essay, usually around 250+ words |

---

### Writing Task 1: Three Related Questions

**Main purpose:**  
To check whether the student can answer personal/general questions clearly, connect ideas, and write with enough detail.

**What is requested:**  
The student receives **three questions on the same topic**. The questions are connected, so the answers should not feel random or disconnected.

> Teaching note: The official format does not fix Question 1, Question 2, and Question 3 as always having the same function. The breakdown below is a practical teaching model that works well for MET preparation.

#### Task 1, Question 1

| Area | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Typical request | Give a direct personal answer or describe a personal experience related to the topic. |
| Student should do | Answer the question directly, give context, and add one or two specific details. |
| Common language needed | Past simple, present simple, basic connectors, personal examples. |
| Good answer shape | **Direct answer → detail → small explanation** |
| Common mistake | Giving only one short sentence with no development. |

**Example of what may be requested:**  
- Describe a time when you had to learn something new.  
- Talk about a place you enjoy visiting.  
- Explain what kind of activities you usually do with friends.

---

#### Task 1, Question 2

| Area | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Typical request | Explain reasons, preferences, feelings, or opinions connected to the same topic. |
| Student should do | Give a clear reason and support it with an example or explanation. |
| Common language needed | Because, since, for example, however, I prefer, I believe, in my opinion. |
| Good answer shape | **Answer → reason → support** |
| Common mistake | Repeating the same idea from Question 1 without expanding it. |

**Example of what may be requested:**  
- Why was this experience important to you?  
- What do you like or dislike about this situation?  
- Why do people usually enjoy this kind of activity?

---

#### Task 1, Question 3

| Area | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Typical request | Extend the topic by giving advice, discussing future plans, comparing options, or explaining a possible effect. |
| Student should do | Develop the answer more fully and show control of organization. |
| Common language needed | Should, would, could, will, might, on the other hand, as a result. |
| Good answer shape | **Position/idea → explanation → example or consequence** |
| Common mistake | Ending too quickly or not answering the exact question. |

**Example of what may be requested:**  
- What advice would you give to someone in this situation?  
- How might this situation change in the future?  
- What are the advantages or disadvantages of this activity?

---

### Writing Task 1: Teacher Checklist

Use this checklist when reviewing student answers:

- Does the student answer **all three questions**?
- Is each answer more than one isolated sentence?
- Are ideas connected with simple but clear linking words?
- Does the student include specific details?
- Is the grammar accurate enough for the meaning to be clear?
- Is the vocabulary appropriate for the topic?
- Does the student avoid copying the same structure three times?

---

### Writing Task 2: Essay

**Main purpose:**  
To check whether the student can write a formal, organized essay with clear ideas, reasons, and support.

| Area | Explanation |
|---|---|
| What is requested | Write an essay responding to one prompt. |
| Expected style | More formal than Task 1. |
| Expected structure | Introduction, body paragraphs, conclusion. |
| Expected support | Reasons, examples, explanations, consequences, comparisons. |
| Typical length | Usually 250+ words. |
| Main skill | Clear argument and organization. |

#### What the student must do

The student should:

1. Understand the prompt.
2. Take a clear position or answer the question directly.
3. Organize ideas into multiple paragraphs.
4. Support ideas with reasons and examples.
5. Use formal or semi-formal language.
6. End with a clear conclusion.

#### Recommended essay structure

```text
Introduction
- Paraphrase the topic.
- Give a clear opinion or main idea.

Body Paragraph 1
- First main reason.
- Explanation.
- Example or consequence.

Body Paragraph 2
- Second main reason.
- Explanation.
- Example or consequence.

Conclusion
- Restate the main idea.
- Give a final comment.
```

#### Common Writing Task 2 prompts

MET essay prompts usually focus on general topics, such as:

- education
- work
- technology
- health
- community
- transportation
- environment
- personal habits
- public services
- lifestyle
- communication

#### Common mistakes in Writing Task 2

- Writing one long paragraph.
- Giving opinions without support.
- Not answering the prompt exactly.
- Using informal spoken language.
- Repeating the same words too often.
- Using memorized phrases that do not fit the topic.
- Writing too little.

## 3. Speaking Section

### General format

The MET Speaking section has **5 tasks** and lasts about **10 minutes**. Responses are recorded digitally and scored by trained raters.

| Task | Time | What is requested |
|---|---:|---|
| Task 1 | 60 seconds | Describe a picture |
| Task 2 | 60 seconds | Talk about a personal experience |
| Task 3 | 60 seconds | Express a personal opinion |
| Task 4 | 90 seconds | Discuss advantages and disadvantages |
| Task 5 | 90 seconds | Explain and justify an opinion to a person of authority |

---

### Speaking Task 1: Describe a Picture

| Area | Explanation |
|---|---|
| What is requested | Describe what is happening in the picture. |
| Student should mention | People, place, actions, objects, possible situation, and simple inferences. |
| Best structure | **General scene → people/actions → details → possible inference** |
| Main skill | Clear description under time pressure. |
| Common mistake | Only listing objects without explaining the situation. |

#### Useful sentence frames

- This picture shows...
- In the foreground, I can see...
- In the background, there is/there are...
- The people seem to be...
- They might be...
- It looks like...

---

### Speaking Task 2: Personal Experience

| Area | Explanation |
|---|---|
| What is requested | Talk about a personal experience related to the picture/topic. |
| Student should do | Tell a short story with enough detail. |
| Best structure | **When/where → what happened → result/feeling** |
| Main skill | Narrating past events clearly. |
| Common mistake | Speaking generally instead of telling a specific experience. |

#### Useful sentence frames

- I remember a time when...
- This happened when I was...
- At first...
- After that...
- In the end...
- I felt... because...

---

### Speaking Task 3: Personal Opinion

| Area | Explanation |
|---|---|
| What is requested | Give an opinion about a topic related to the picture. |
| Student should do | State an opinion and support it with reasons. |
| Best structure | **Opinion → reason 1 → reason 2/example → final comment** |
| Main skill | Explaining a viewpoint clearly. |
| Common mistake | Giving an opinion without explaining why. |

#### Useful sentence frames

- In my opinion...
- I believe that...
- The main reason is...
- Another reason is...
- For example...
- That is why I think...

---

### Speaking Task 4: Advantages and Disadvantages

| Area | Explanation |
|---|---|
| What is requested | Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of a situation. |
| Student should do | Present both sides in a balanced way. |
| Best structure | **Introduction → advantages → disadvantages → balanced conclusion** |
| Main skill | Comparing ideas and organizing contrast. |
| Common mistake | Only talking about one side. |

#### Useful sentence frames

- There are several advantages to this.
- One benefit is...
- Another positive point is...
- However, there are also disadvantages.
- One problem is...
- On balance, I think...

---

### Speaking Task 5: Persuasion / Opinion to a Person of Authority

| Area | Explanation |
|---|---|
| What is requested | Explain and justify an opinion to someone with authority. |
| Student should do | Persuade the listener with clear arguments and respectful language. |
| Best structure | **Recommendation → reasons → benefits → respectful closing** |
| Main skill | Persuasive speaking. |
| Common mistake | Sounding too aggressive, too informal, or not giving enough support. |

#### Useful sentence frames

- I strongly believe that...
- I would recommend...
- This would be beneficial because...
- One important reason is...
- This change could help...
- I understand there may be concerns, but...
- For these reasons, I hope you will consider this idea.

## 4. Listening Section

### General format

The MET Listening section lasts about **35 minutes** and has **50 multiple-choice questions**.

| Part | Question count | What the student hears | What is requested |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Part 1 | 19 questions | Short conversations between two people | Answer one question after each conversation |
| Part 2 | 14 questions | Four longer conversations | Answer three to five questions for each conversation |
| Part 3 | 17 questions | Four short talks by one speaker | Answer three to four questions for each talk |

---

### Listening Part 1: Short Conversations

| Area | Explanation |
|---|---|
| What is requested | Understand the main point, speaker intention, detail, or implied meaning in a short exchange. |
| Student should listen for | Who is speaking, where they are, what the problem is, what the speaker means. |
| Main skill | Quick comprehension. |
| Common mistake | Choosing an answer based on one word instead of the whole meaning. |

#### Typical question focus

- Main idea
- Specific detail
- Speaker attitude
- Implied meaning
- Function of a phrase
- What will happen next

---

### Listening Part 2: Longer Conversations

| Area | Explanation |
|---|---|
| What is requested | Understand longer conversations and answer several questions about the same audio. |
| Student should listen for | Main topic, sequence, problem, solution, speaker opinions, details. |
| Main skill | Maintaining comprehension over a longer audio. |
| Common mistake | Forgetting earlier information by the time the questions appear. |

#### Typical question focus

- Why someone is calling or speaking
- What problem is discussed
- What solution is suggested
- What the speakers agree or disagree about
- What a speaker will probably do next

---

### Listening Part 3: Short Talks

| Area | Explanation |
|---|---|
| What is requested | Understand short talks, announcements, lectures, or informational messages. |
| Student should listen for | Purpose, main idea, important details, organization, speaker attitude. |
| Main skill | Understanding one-speaker information. |
| Common mistake | Missing the purpose of the talk and focusing only on isolated details. |

#### Typical question focus

- Main purpose
- Topic
- Key detail
- Reason
- Speaker attitude
- Inference
- Next step or recommendation

---

## 5. Reading Section

### General format

The MET Reading section lasts about **65 minutes** and has **50 questions**. It includes grammar and reading comprehension.

| Part | Question count | What the student sees | What is requested |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Part 1 | 20 questions | Sentences with a missing word or phrase | Choose the correct grammar/vocabulary option |
| Part 2 | 10 questions | Two single reading texts | Answer five questions for each text |
| Part 3 | 20 questions | Two sets of three related texts | Answer ten questions for each set |

---

### Reading Part 1: Grammar

| Area | Explanation |
|---|---|
| What is requested | Complete a sentence by choosing the correct word or phrase. |
| Student should check | Grammar, meaning, collocation, verb tense, prepositions, connectors. |
| Main skill | Accuracy and sentence-level control. |
| Common mistake | Choosing an option that sounds familiar but does not fit grammatically. |

#### Common grammar areas

- Verb tense
- Subject-verb agreement
- Articles
- Prepositions
- Comparatives and superlatives
- Modals
- Conditionals
- Relative clauses
- Gerunds and infinitives
- Connectors
- Word form
- Collocations

---

### Reading Part 2: Single-Text Reading

| Area | Explanation |
|---|---|
| What is requested | Read one informational text and answer five questions. |
| Student should do | Identify the main idea, details, reference words, vocabulary in context, and implied meaning. |
| Main skill | Reading comprehension of one text. |
| Common mistake | Reading too slowly and spending too much time on one text. |

#### Typical question focus

- Main idea
- Detail
- Vocabulary in context
- Reference words
- Inference
- Author purpose
- Organization

---

### Reading Part 3: Multiple-Text Reading

| Area | Explanation |
|---|---|
| What is requested | Read three related texts and answer questions across the set. |
| Student should do | Compare information, connect ideas across texts, identify agreement/disagreement, and infer meaning. |
| Main skill | Synthesizing information from multiple sources. |
| Common mistake | Treating the three texts separately and missing cross-text questions. |

#### Typical question focus

- Which text says a specific idea
- How two texts are similar or different
- What the writers agree or disagree about
- Which option is supported by the texts
- Inference across texts
- Purpose of each text

## 7. Student-Facing Summary

The MET checks whether you can use English in real-life situations. You do not need special knowledge about the topics. You need to understand conversations and texts, write clear answers, and speak with enough organization and support.

### What to remember

- In **Writing Task 1**, answer all three questions clearly.
- In **Writing Task 2**, write a formal essay with multiple paragraphs.
- In **Speaking Task 1**, describe the picture clearly.
- In **Speaking Task 2**, tell a personal story.
- In **Speaking Task 3**, give and support your opinion.
- In **Speaking Task 4**, discuss both advantages and disadvantages.
- In **Speaking Task 5**, persuade someone respectfully.
- In **Listening**, focus on main ideas, details, and implied meaning.
- In **Reading**, manage your time and compare information carefully.
- In **Grammar**, choose the option that fits both meaning and structure.

## 8. Teacher Quick Diagnostic Questions

Use these questions after a mock test or practice task.

### Writing

- Did the student answer the exact question?
- Did the student develop ideas with support?
- Is the organization clear?
- Are grammar errors blocking meaning?
- Is the vocabulary appropriate for B2/B2+?

### Speaking

- Did the student complete the function of the task?
- Was the answer organized?
- Did the student support opinions with reasons/examples?
- Was pronunciation clear enough?
- Did hesitation affect communication?

### Listening

- Does the student miss details or main ideas?
- Does the student struggle with inference?
- Does the student get distracted by similar words in the options?
- Does the student lose information in longer audios?

### Reading

- Does the student understand main ideas quickly?
- Does the student find details efficiently?
- Does the student infer meaning from context?
- Does the student compare information across texts?
- Does grammar weakness affect answers?

## 10. Teacher Note

For lesson design, the most important difference between the MET tasks is the **communication function**:

| Skill | Low-level practice | Strong B2/B2+ practice |
|---|---|---|
| Writing | Answer the question | Develop and connect ideas |
| Speaking | Speak for the time | Organize, support, and sound natural |
| Listening | Catch words | Understand meaning, attitude, and inference |
| Reading | Find information | Compare, infer, and synthesize |

A strong MET class should not only practice English. It should train students to understand **what each task is asking them to do**.
